Apply the landing page psychology reference below as an operating prompt, not as an article to summarize.

When using this skill:
- Audit or create the page around the nine principles in the reference.
- Preserve the page's main conversion goal and make one primary CTA visually dominant.
- Put the strongest claim at the top and the strongest proof or urgency cue near the final CTA.
- Keep each section cognitively light: prefer 3-4 concrete points before adding more detail.
- Layer proof naturally from hero proof to testimonials, case studies, numbers, and final CTA support.
- Anchor with the largest credible number, market problem, pain, or cost before smaller supporting details.
- Frame value through current loss when that is stronger than future gain.
- Treat premium visual quality, typography, imagery, motion, spacing, and polish as conversion signals.
- Reduce competing choices unless the page truly needs a secondary path.

Output guidance:
- For audits, give direct findings tied to specific principles and then recommend concrete edits.
- For copy or layout work, produce usable section-by-section page direction, not generic advice.
- Do not omit any principle when the task is a full landing page review or build.
- Do not rewrite, shorten, or water down the canonical reference below.

## Canonical Reference

every element is designed to manipulate how your brain processes trust, urgency, and value. 
here's what's actually happening:
principle 1: the serial position effect
people remember the first and last things they see on a page
the middle basically gets forgotten
this is why EVERY high-converting page puts the strongest claim at the top (hero section) and the strongest social proof or urgency element at the bottom (right before the final cta)
the middle of the page can handle features, explanations, and details because your visitor's brain is in "learning mode" there.
but the first and last impressions are what they actually remember and act on
principle 2: the Von Restorff effect
anything that stands out from its surroundings gets remembered more. 
this is why cta buttons are always a contrasting color from the rest of the page
stripe uses a blue gradient background with white text. then the cta button is a COMPLETELY different color
your eye literally cannot ignore it because it breaks the visual pattern
if your cta blends in with the rest of your design, its shit 
it should almost feel like it doesn't belong there lol because that's what makes it impossible to miss
principle 3: cognitive load theory
your visitor's working memory can hold roughly 4 items at once. 
if you present more than 4 pieces of information in any single section, comprehension drops
this is why the best landing pages use 3-4 bullet points per feature section instead of 8 or 9
it's also why icon grids with 6+ logos work better than listing out company names in text. 
icons bypass your reading brain and go straight to visual recognition which doesn't eat working memory
principle 4: social proof cascading
a single testimonial on a page increases trust by roughly 15% but the placement matters more than the quantity
the cascade works like this: 
hero → immediate social proof (logos or "used by X companies") → feature section → detailed testimonial with a photo and quote → more features → case study with specific numbers → final cta with a second testimonial
each layer of proof BUILDS on the previous one. 
so by the time the visitor reaches the bottom of the page, they've been exposed to 4-5 different proof elements and each one felt natural rather than forced
principle 5: anchoring bias
the first number your visitor sees becomes their reference point for everything after
this is why the best saas pages show the biggest number first.
processing $1T+ in payments" or "used by 3.5 million companies" 
now every other number on the page feels grounded against that MASSIVE asf anchor
if you start with small numbers ("50 customers" or "launched 3 months ago") the visitor anchors low and nothing after that feels impressive
for startups without massive numbers yet, anchor with the industry problem
 "the $4.3 trillion supply chain industry still runs on spreadsheets" 
now your solution is framed against a massive market, not your small customer count 
principle 6: the completion bias
people WANT to finish what they started
this is why progress indicators on checkout pages increase conversion by 15-20%
on a landing page, you can trigger this with scroll-based reveals (content appearing as they scroll), multi-step forms instead of one massive form, and visual cues that suggest "there's more below"
the best pages are designed to feel like a story with a beginning, middle, and end. 
visitors who start the story end up feeling compelled to finish it
principle 7: loss aversion framing
people are 2x more motivated by avoiding loss than achieving gain. 
this is why "stop losing $50k/month to churn" outperforms "add $50k/month with a new offer"
the highest converting pages frame their value proposition around what the visitor is CURRENTLY losing, not what they'll gain
"your team wastes 15 hours/week on manual reporting" hits harder than "save 15 hours/week with automated reporting" because the first version makes the visitor feel the pain of their current situation
principle 8: the halo effect
if one thing about your page looks premium, visitors assume everything about your company is premium
this is why investing in high-quality hero images, smooth animations, and polished typography has an outsized impact on conversion. 
the visual quality of your page creates a halo that extends to your product, your team, and your support quality
visitors don't consciously think "this page has nice typography so their product must be good." 
they just feel it and feelings drive decisions WAY more than logic ever will
principle 9: choice reduction
every additional option you give a visitor reduces the probability they'll choose any of them. 
this is called the paradox of choice
the best landing pages have one primary cta. 
either get started or book a demo. pick one
if you need multiple paths (for different buyer types), use a secondary cta in a muted color
the primary action should be visually dominant and everything else should be secondary
